# **Introduction to Section 7: The Need for Specular Reflection**

Section 7 represents the critical extension from the matte, view-independent Lambertian model to glossy, view-dependent specular reflection. While Section 6 successfully modeled diffuse surfaces like painted walls, paper, and rough stone, it fundamentally cannot capture the bright highlights visible on smooth or polished materials—the white gleam on a red apple, the shine on polished metal, the glint on plastic surfaces, or the reflection on glass. These highlights are specular reflections, where light bounces <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">preferentially in directions near the mirror reflection angle rather than scattering uniformly.</mark> The Blinn-Phong model, introduced by <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">Jim Blinn in 1977</mark> as an improvement over Bui Tuong Phong's original 1975 model, provides a computationally efficient approximation to this physically complex phenomenon. Understanding this section is crucial because <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">specular highlights</mark> are what make computer-generated objects look three-dimensional, tactile, and realistic—without them, even geometrically correct models appear flat and artificial. Moreover, Section 7 introduces the halfway vector, a geometric construction that elegantly simplifies the reflection calculation while maintaining physical plausibility, <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">demonstrating how clever mathematical reformulations can achieve both efficiency and accuracy</mark>.

# **7.1 Motivation: Why Phong Wasn't Good Enough**

The motivation subsection establishes why you need a new model beyond Lambertian diffuse shading and why specifically Blinn's modification improves over Phong's original approach. The fundamental physical phenomenon being addressed is that smooth or glossy surfaces concentrate reflected energy near the specular or mirror reflection direction. Unlike Lambertian surfaces where light scatters uniformly regardless of where you look, glossy surfaces<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;"> exhibit view-dependent reflection</mark>—move your head relative to a glossy object under fixed lighting, and you'll see highlights appear, move, and disappear as your viewing angle changes. This is why rotating a shiny apple under a lamp creates a moving bright spot that follows your movement.

Phong's original 1975 model captured this by computing the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">perfect mirror reflection vector r-hat</mark>, defined geometrically as r-hat equals 2(n-hat dot l-hat) times n-hat minus l-hat. This vector represents the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">direction where light would perfectly reflect off the surface</mark> if it were a perfect mirror. Phong then measured <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">how close the view direction v-hat was to this reflection direction</mark> using the dot product r-hat dot v-hat, <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">raising it to a power s to create a lobe of decreasing intensity around the perfect reflection angle</mark>. The <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">larger the exponent s, the tighter the lobe and the more mirror-like the surface appearance</mark>.

However, Blinn identified two significant problems with Phong's reflection-vector approach. First, computational cost: computing the reflection vector r-hat requires a <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">vector subtraction, a dot product, and a scalar-vector multiplication</mark>—not enormous, but non-trivial when performed billions of times per frame across millions of pixels. Second, and more seriously, <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">numerical instability at grazing angles</mark>: when the view direction v-hat approaches the surface tangent plane (perpendicular to the normal), the reflection vector calculation becomes ill-conditioned and can produce discontinuities or artifacts. As you tilt your view to near-parallel with the surface, the reflection vector swings wildly, potentially causing visual popping or flickering.

Blinn's 1977 innovation was to replace the reflection vector with the halfway vector h-hat, defined <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">as the normalized sum of the light direction and view direction</mark>: h-hat equals (l-hat plus v-hat) divided by the norm of (l-hat plus v-hat). This halfway vector bisects the angle between the light and view directions. The key <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">geometric insight is that when the halfway vector aligns with the surface normal (h-hat equals n-hat), the viewer is positioned exactly to see the perfect mirror reflection of the light source</mark>. The beauty of this formulation is that it avoids explicit reflection-vector calculation entirely—<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">you simply add two unit vectors and normalize.</mark> The resulting model is both cheaper to compute (one vector addition, one normalization, one dot product) and <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">better behaved numerically at all viewing angles, with no discontinuities as the view direction changes</mark>.

Your motivation subsection should emphasize this historical context: Phong gave us the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">conceptual framework</mark> (view-dependent specular lobes with power-law falloff), but <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">Blinn gave us the practical implementation that's actually used in real-time rendering</mark>. The Blinn-Phong model isn't just "Phong with a tweak"—it's a fundamentally better parameterization of the same underlying physics, demonstrating how mathematical reformulation can simultaneously improve both computational efficiency and numerical stability. This is a recurring theme in applied mathematics: finding the right variables to work with can make hard problems tractable.

# **7.2 The Halfway Vector: Geometric Intuition and Mathematical Definition**

The halfway vector subsection provides the geometric and algebraic foundation for the entire Blinn-Phong model. Given normalized light direction l-hat and view direction v-hat (both unit vectors by construction, as specified in Section 3.3), the halfway vector is defined by Equation 14: h-hat equals (l-hat plus v-hat) divided by the norm of (l-hat plus v-hat). Let's unpack why this definition is geometrically meaningful and how it relates to specular reflection.

**Geometric interpretation:** Imagine standing at a surface point looking toward the camera (along v-hat) while light arrives from the source (along l-hat). The halfway vector h-hat points exactly halfway between these two directions—if you were to rotate from l-hat toward v-hat, <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">stopping halfway through the angular sweep, you'd be pointing along h-hat</mark>. This bisection property is fundamental. Now, here's the critical insight: if the surface normal n-hat happened to align perfectly with this halfway vector (n-hat equals h-hat), then by the law of reflection, light arriving along l-hat would bounce perfectly along v-hat. In other words, you'd be positioned to see the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">exact specular reflection of the light source</mark>. Conversely, as the normal deviates from the halfway vector, you're increasingly off the perfect reflection angle, and <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">the specular contribution should decrease</mark>.

**Mathematical construction:** Starting with two unit vectors l-hat and v-hat, their sum <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">l-hat plus v-hat points in the direction that bisects them</mark>, but its magnitude is not necessarily one. In fact, by the parallelogram rule, the magnitude depends on the angle between l-hat and v-hat: if they're parallel (pointing the same direction), the magnitude is 2; if they're perpendicular, the magnitude is sqrt(2); if they're opposite, the magnitude approaches zero (though this case is geometrically impossible in your model since both point away from the surface). <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">Normalizing this sum—dividing by its magnitude—gives you a unit vector h-hat pointing along the bisection direction.</mark>

**The specular half-angle alpha:** The angle α (alpha) <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">between the surface normal n-hat and the halfway vector h-hat is called the specular half-angle</mark>, defined via Equation 8 from Section 3.3: cos α equals n-hat dot h-hat. When α equals zero (h-hat perfectly aligned with n-hat), you're at <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">maximum specular response—the viewer sees the perfect mirror reflection</mark>. As α increases (h-hat deviates from n-hat), you're increasingly off the specular direction and the highlight intensity should fall off. The Blinn-Phong model captures this falloff by <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">raising cos α to a power</mark>, exactly analogous to how Phong raised the angle to the reflection vector to a power, but with simpler geometry.

**Why this works computationally:** Computing h-hat requires one vector addition (l-hat plus v-hat), one norm calculation (square root of the sum of three squared components), and three scalar divisions (dividing each component by the norm). The total cost is roughly <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">10-12 floating-point operations.</mark> In contrast, Phong's reflection vector requires computing 2(n-hat dot l-hat) times n-hat minus l-hat, which involves a dot product (3 multiplications plus 2 additions), a scalar-vector multiplication (3 multiplications), and a vector subtraction (3 subtractions)—comparable cost but with worse numerical conditioning. Moreover, once you've computed h-hat, <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">the specular calculation reduces to a single dot product (n-hat dot h-hat) and one power operation, making per-light evaluation extremely fast.</mark>

**Reciprocity consideration:** An elegant property of the halfway vector formulation is that it's symmetric in l-hat and v-hat: swapping the light and view directions gives the same halfway vector (because addition commutes). <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">This symmetry corresponds to Helmholtz reciprocity, the physical principle that light paths are reversible</mark>—if you can see the light from position A, you could illuminate position A from the light's original position. While the basic Blinn-Phong shader equation (15) doesn't fully enforce reciprocity (because it's not normalized), the BRDF formulation in subsection 7.5 does, connecting the model to rigorous physics.

Your presentation of subsection 7.2 should emphasize that the halfway vector is not an arbitrary mathematical trick—<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">it has clear geometric meaning as the bisector of light and view directions, and aligning the normal with this bisector is the precise condition for perfect specular reflection</mark>. This geometric intuition makes the subsequent mathematical development natural and physically motivated rather than appearing as empirical curve-fitting.

# **7.3 Specular Shader Equation: From Geometry to Implementation**

The specular shader equation subsection translates the halfway vector geometry into a concrete computational formula. Equation 15 presents the Blinn-Phong specular term: Cspec(x) equals the sum from j equals 1 to NL of ks Hadamard-product Ij times max(n-hat dot hj-hat, zero) raised to the power s. Let's dissect every component of this equation because each element encodes physical meaning or practical necessity.

**The sum over lights:** Just as in the Lambertian equation (12), you <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">sum over all NL light</mark> sources because in your local illumination model (stated in Section 4.4, assumption 3), each light contributes independently with no inter-reflection or shadowing. This linear superposition is valid because Maxwell's equations (which govern light propagation) are linear in the absence of nonlinear media. The consequence is that your computational complexity remains O(NL)—linear in the number of lights—making real-time rendering tractable even with dozens of lights.

**The specular coefficient ks:** This is an RGB triple (three-component vector) representing the material's specular reflectance. For physically based materials, ks typically represents the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">Fresnel reflectance at normal incidence</mark>, which depends on the material's index of refraction. Dielectric materials (glass, plastic, water) have low specular coefficients (ks around 0.04 for typical plastics), while metallic materials have high specular coefficients (ks around 0.5-1.0 for metals, which is why metals look shiny). The specular color ks is often white or gray for dielectrics (the highlight color matches the light color) but colored for metals (gold has yellowish highlights, copper has reddish highlights). The Hadamard product ks ⊙ Ij means the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">material's specular color modulates the light's color component-wise</mark>, allowing for colored highlights when either the material or light has chromatic content.

**The geometric term max(n-hat dot hj-hat, 0):** This is the cosine of the specular half-angle α, clamped to non-negative values. The max(·, 0) clamp serves the same purpose as in the Lambertian term—it e<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">nforces that lights geometrically below the surface horizon contribute zero illumination</mark>. Negative dot products would correspond to the halfway vector pointing below the surface, which is physically meaningless in your local surface model. The cosine term captures how aligned the normal is with the halfway vector: perfect alignment (α equals zero) gives cos α equals one, while perpendicularity (α equals 90 degrees) gives cos α equals zero.

**The specular exponent s:** This positive scalar (s greater than zero) <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">controls the size and intensity of the specular lobe</mark>—how "shiny" the surface appears. Raising cos α to power s creates a narrow lobe concentrated around α equals zero. Small exponents (s equals 1 to 10) produce wide, broad highlights characteristic of rough or matte surfaces like unpolished plastic or painted metal. <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">Large exponents (s equals 100 to 1000) produce tight, pinpoint highlights characteristic of highly polished surfaces like mirrors, chrome, or glossy paint</mark>. The exponent s is often called the "shininess" or "glossiness" parameter, and it's the primary <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">artistic control for specular appearance</mark>. Subsection 7.4 will quantify this relationship precisely, but intuitively, larger s means faster falloff away from perfect alignment, hence smaller highlight.

**Computational evaluation:** For each light j, you compute: (1) the halfway vector hj-hat from lj-hat and v-hat (<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">one vector addition plus normalization</mark>), (2) the dot product n-hat dot hj-hat (<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">three multiplications plus two additions</mark>), (3) the clamp to zero (<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">one comparison plus conditional</mark>), (4) the power operation to compute (cos α)^s (<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">one exponentiation</mark>), and (5) the RGB multiplication ks ⊙ Ij times the power result (<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">three multiplications</mark>). The power operation can be computed efficiently using hardware instructions or approximated with polynomial expansions for even greater speed. The <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">total cost per light is roughly 15-20 floating-point operations</mark>—trivial for modern GPUs executing thousands of pixels in parallel.

**View dependence:** Notice that <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">v-hat appears implicitly through the halfway vector hj-hat, making the specular term view-dependent</mark> unlike the Lambertian diffuse term which was view-independent. This means that as the camera moves around a scene with fixed lighting and geometry, the diffuse appearance remains constant but the specular highlights slide across surfaces following the changing view angles. This is precisely the behavior you observe when walking around a car under fixed overhead lights—the glossy paint shows highlights that track your movement. The computational implication is that <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">specular shading must be recalculated every frame as the camera moves, while diffuse shading could potentially be cached if the lighting doesn't change</mark> (though in practice, both are recomputed per frame for simplicity).

**Physical units and normalization:** Equation 15 as stated is the shader equation used in <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">practical implementations—the formula that actually appears in GPU code</mark>. However, it's not properly normalized to conserve energy. The raw form produces specular values that, when integrated over the hemisphere, may exceed the incident light intensity for large s values, violating energy conservation. Subsection 7.5 will introduce the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">normalized BRDF formulation with a correction factor</mark> (s plus 2) divided by (2π), which ensures that no matter how large s becomes, the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">total reflected energy never exceeds the incident energy.</mark> Most real-time rendering uses the unnormalized form (15) for speed, accepting slight energy non-conservation, while physically based rendering uses the normalized form for accuracy. Your report presents both, showing you understand the trade-off between practical efficiency and physical rigor.

# **7.5 BRDF Formulation of the Specular Term: Physics Meets Rigor**

The BRDF formulation subsection elevates the Blinn-Phong model from a practical shader equation to a physically grounded reflection model by recasting it in terms of the Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF). This is where your report connects computational graphics to rigorous physics and optical theory.

**BRDF definition and physical meaning:** The BRDF, denoted fr(l-hat, v-hat) with units of inverse steradians (sr^(-1)), is a fundamental radiometric quantity that relates incoming radiance to outgoing radiance at a surface point. Specifically, if radiance Li arrives from direction l-hat (ωi in the general notation), the differential radiance scattered toward direction v-hat (ωo) is dLo equals fr(l-hat, v-hat) times Li times cos θi times dω, where dω is the differential solid angle around l-hat. Integrating this over all incoming directions gives the total outgoing radiance, which is exactly the Rendering Equation that will appear in Section 9. The BRDF encapsulates all surface reflection properties in a single function—it's the "DNA" of the material's appearance.

**The three physical requirements for a valid BRDF:** Any physically realizable BRDF must satisfy three fundamental properties derived from physical laws and thermodynamics. First, **positivity**: fr(l-hat, v-hat) must be greater than or equal to zero for all directions. Negative reflection is physically meaningless—<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">surfaces cannot emit "negative radiance."</mark> This seems obvious but is important to verify for any proposed BRDF. Second, **<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">Helmholtz reciprocity</mark>**: fr(ωi, ωo) equals fr(ωo, ωi), meaning swapping incoming and outgoing directions gives the same BRDF value. This follows from time-reversal symmetry of Maxwell's equations and has deep connections to detailed balance in statistical mechanics. Reciprocity means that if you can see a light from position A, you could see position A if the light were placed there—light paths are reversible. Third, **<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">energy conservation</mark>**: the integral of fr(ωi, ωo) times cos θo over the outgoing hemisphere must be <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">at most 1 for every incoming direction ωi</mark>. This is Equation 27, which appears in Section 9.5 and will be verified explicitly. Energy conservation states that reflected power cannot exceed incident power—surfaces cannot create energy from nothing, only redirect existing light.

**From delta function to smooth lobe:** For an ideal perfect mirror, the BRDF is a <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">Dirac delta function</mark>: fr equals δ(ωo minus ωr), where ωr is the perfect reflection direction. All incoming light from direction ωi gets reflected into exactly one outgoing direction ωr equals 2(n-hat dot ωi) n-hat minus ωi, with infinite intensity at that direction and zero everywhere else. Real smooth surfaces aren't perfect mirrors—microscopic roughness causes slight spreading around the specular direction. The Blinn-Phong model <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">approximates this spreading</mark> with a smooth lobe (cos α)^s that concentrates around the mirror direction but has finite width. The larger s, the closer the approximation to the delta function (perfect mirror limit).

**The normalized Blinn-Phong BRDF (Equation 17):** The properly normalized form is fr^BP(l-hat, v-hat) equals ks times (s plus 2) divided by (2π) times max(n-hat dot h-hat, 0)^s. This is the BRDF that satisfies energy conservation. Let's parse this formula. The term ks is the specular coefficient (typically a scalar or RGB triple) representing the fraction of incident light that gets specularly reflected versus absorbed or diffusely scattered. The term max(n-hat dot h-hat, 0)^s is the lobe shape you've already seen. The crucial addition is the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">normalization constant (s plus 2)/(2π).</mark> Where does this come from? It's derived by requiring that when you integrate the BRDF over the hemisphere (weighted by cos θ), you get exactly ks, not more. Section 9.5 will show this calculation explicitly: <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">the integral of cos^(s+1)(θ) times sin(θ) dθ from 0 to π/2 equals 1/(s plus 2), and integrating over the full azimuthal range (0 to 2π) gives 2π/(s plus 2)</mark>. To make the hemispherical integral equal ks, you must multiply by (s plus 2)/(2π), canceling these factors. The beauty is that <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">this normalization works for any s</mark>—whether you have a broad lobe (s equals 1) or a tight lobe (s equals 1000), the energy conservation integral gives the same result: ks.

**Why two formulations (shader equation vs. BRDF)?** Equation 15 (the shader equation) is the <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">unnormalized form actually used in most real-time engines—it omits the (s plus 2)/(2π) factor for speed and simplicity.</mark> Equation 17 (the BRDF form) includes the normalization and is used in <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">physically based renderers where energy conservation is critical for photorealistic results</mark>. The unnormalized form produces slightly incorrect brightnesses that vary with s, but the error is often visually acceptable and the performance gain matters for real-time. The normalized form ensures consistent energy balance, <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">crucial when rendering multi-bounce indirect illumination where small errors compound</mark>. Your report presenting both shows sophistication—you know the practical implementation and the physical ideal.

**Connecting to microfacet theory:** Modern physically based rendering models like Cook-Torrance (mentioned in Section 4.2) are microfacet models where the BRDF is derived from statistical distributions of microscopic surface facets. The Blinn-Phong BRDF can be viewed as an empirical approximation to a microfacet model where the normal distribution function is (s plus 2)/(2π) times cos^s(α). This connection shows that Blinn-Phong, <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">while originally empirical, has a physical interpretation in terms of surface microstructure. </mark>The exponent s relates inversely to surface roughness—high s means low roughness (many facets aligned with the macroscopic normal), low s means high roughness (facets randomly oriented).

# **7.7 Advantages and Limitations: Critical Assessment**

The advantages and limitations subsection provides honest evaluation of the Blinn-Phong specular model, acknowledging its strengths for real-time applications while recognizing its physical limitations. This critical thinking demonstrates scientific maturity—understanding that models are purpose-built tools, not universal truths.

**Advantages:** The Blinn-Phong model has several compelling strengths that explain its dominance in real-time rendering for nearly five decades. First, **computational efficiency**: as analyzed in subsection 7.3, the model requires only a handful of operations per light—vector addition, normalization, dot product, power function, and RGB multiplication, totaling perhaps 20 floating-point operations. On modern GPUs executing thousands of fragments in parallel, this is effectively free. Compare this to physically based microfacet models like Cook-Torrance which require evaluating multiple terms (normal distribution, geometric shadowing, Fresnel reflectance) involving transcendental functions—substantially more expensive. Second, **simplicity of implementation**: the model is straightforward to code, with no complex data structures, no approximations to manage, and no edge cases to handle. The entire specular calculation fits in 3-4 lines of shader code. Third, **native hardware support**: the Blinn-Phong model is directly supported in fixed-function graphics pipelines (older OpenGL, DirectX 7-9 era) and remains the default in many engines even after programmable shaders became standard. Countless tutorials, examples, and production codebases use Blinn-Phong, providing a vast ecosystem of knowledge and tools. Fourth, **intuitive artist control**: the exponent s provides a single meaningful parameter (shininess) that artists can adjust with immediate, predictable visual feedback. Increase s, highlights tighten; decrease s, highlights broaden. This simplicity beats physically based models where parameters like "roughness alpha" require understanding of microfacet theory to tune effectively.

**Limitations:** Despite these advantages, the Blinn-Phong model has fundamental physical limitations that restrict its use in photorealistic rendering. First, **energy non-conservation in the unnormalized form**: Equation 15, as used in most real-time implementations, lacks the (s plus 2)/(2π) normalization factor. This means the total energy reflected varies with s—surfaces become overall brighter as you increase shininess, which is physically incorrect. The energy should remain constant (equal to ks times the incident energy) regardless of how the lobe concentrates. While the normalized form (Equation 17) fixes this, many engines still use the unnormalized version for historical reasons and slight performance advantage. Second, **no Fresnel effect**: Real materials exhibit Fresnel reflectance—they become more reflective at grazing angles regardless of surface roughness. Look at a flat surface like a floor or table: at normal incidence (looking straight down), you see mostly the diffuse body color; at grazing incidence (looking almost parallel to the surface), you see strong specular reflection even on matte surfaces. This is why wet roads appear more reflective at shallow viewing angles, and why the horizon of the ocean is bright even though water isn't very reflective at perpendicular incidence. The Blinn-Phong model treats ks as constant for all viewing angles, missing this crucial effect. Physically based models incorporate Fresnel terms F(θ) that modulate reflectance based on viewing angle, dramatically improving realism, especially for dielectric materials.

Third, **empirical basis**: While subsection 7.5 connected Blinn-Phong to BRDF formalism, the model was originally empirical—Blinn chose the (cos α)^s form because it looked reasonable, not because it was derived from first principles of optics or microfacet theory. More rigorous models like Cook-Torrance derive their BRDF from statistical distributions of microscopic surface facets, providing parameters (normal distribution function, geometric shadowing/masking, Fresnel reflectance) that directly correspond to measurable physical properties. Blinn-Phong's exponent s is a phenomenological parameter—you can't easily relate it to roughness measurements from microscopy or gonioreflectometry. Fourth, **single lobe limitation**: The model assumes a single specular lobe centered on the halfway vector. Real materials, especially anisotropic surfaces like brushed metal, can have multiple lobes or elongated lobes that require more sophisticated models. Fifth, **no inter-reflection or indirect illumination**: This is a limitation of the entire local illumination framework (Section 4.4 assumptions), not specific to Blinn-Phong, but it's worth noting. The model only computes direct light-to-surface-to-eye paths, missing the rich indirect illumination (color bleeding, ambient occlusion, caustics) that dominates realistic appearance.

**Modern context:** Despite these limitations, Blinn-Phong remains widely used in hybrid rendering pipelines. Many engines use Blinn-Phong for real-time preview and editor interaction (because it's fast and provides immediate feedback) while using physically based models for final quality rendering or cinematics. Understanding Blinn-Phong is essential even in the physically based rendering era because it provides intuition for specular reflection generally and because legacy content and codebases still rely on it extensively. Moreover, the halfway vector h-hat that Blinn introduced is still used in modern PBR models—the microfacet normal distribution is often evaluated at h-hat, making Blinn's geometric insight enduringly valuable even as the specific BRDF formulation evolved.
